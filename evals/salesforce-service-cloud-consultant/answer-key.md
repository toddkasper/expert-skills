1. **Competent:** The Entitlement Process milestone timer resolution is driven by the Business Hours record's time granularity. When Business Hours end at exactly 5:00 PM and a case is created at 4:45 PM, the system calculates remaining time in the current business day as 15 minutes, then rolls the remaining 3 hours and 45 minutes of the SLA into the next business day — but floating-point or rounding in the Business Hours engine can introduce a 15-minute offset depending on how sub-hour segments are calculated. The most likely cause is that the Business Hours record has a clock that doesn't align with the target — specifically, the start time is 8:00 AM but the milestone timer calculation for the Friday window is computing the overnight gap as 16 hours (5 PM Friday to 9 AM Monday) rather than using the business-hours-aware carry-forward correctly. Confirm by querying `SELECT TargetDate, StartDate FROM CaseMilestone WHERE CaseId = '<test id>'` and back-calculating 4 business hours from 4:45 PM Friday against the Business Hours record: the expected answer is Monday 8:45 AM, confirming the rounding is happening at the day-boundary transition. **Trap:** Assuming the Entitlement Process is misconfigured or the milestone duration is set to 3 hours and 45 minutes — the duration setting is almost certainly correct; the issue is how the timer handles end-of-day carryover. **Verify:** Create three test cases: one at 3:00 PM (expect target at 5:00 PM same day, clean), one at 4:45 PM Friday, and one at 8:00 AM Monday. Compare the `CaseMilestone.TargetDate` on each against manual business-hours arithmetic to isolate whether the discrepancy is systematic at day boundaries.

2. **Competent:** Email-to-Case uses a **thread ID token** embedded in the subject line and/or body of emails sent from Salesforce to stitch replies back to the existing case. When agents (or the Gmail shared inbox owner) reply from **outside Salesforce** — directly from Gmail — that outbound reply does not carry the Salesforce thread token. When the customer replies to that Gmail-originated message, there is no token for Email-to-Case to match, so Salesforce treats the inbound email as a net-new case. The correct process is: all case replies must originate from **within Salesforce** (via the Send Email action on the Case), so that Salesforce's thread token is embedded in the outgoing message and subsequent customer replies route correctly. **Trap:** Assuming the routing address or email filter is misconfigured — the routing address is working fine (it's creating cases and accepting inbound mail); the problem is the absence of the thread token in Gmail-originated outbound replies. Or proposing a complex email parsing middleware — the fix is a process change, not a technical one. **Verify:** Send a test reply from Salesforce on a known case, inspect the outgoing email's subject for the thread ID token (typically a long alphanumeric in `ref:` format), reply to it as a customer from an external mailbox, and confirm the resulting email appends as a comment rather than creating a new case.

4. **Competent:** Reject the design. 5. **Competent:** Recommend the manager use **Omni Supervisor** — it provides real-time visibility into queue backlog (work item counts by queue), agent presence status (Available, Busy, Away), active routing tasks, and average handle times, all live without manual refresh. The list-view approach has two key shortcomings: (1) it is a **pull/refresh model** — the manager must manually refresh to get updated counts, which is not truly real-time and becomes impractical at any volume; (2) standard list views show case records but do not surface agent availability or routing state. Omni Supervisor is the correct operational tool for live contact center oversight. 6. **Competent:** The design flaw is **DML inside a loop in a Flow** — the Record Create element is executing one DML operation per Case Comment inside the loop iteration. This is the Flow equivalent of SOQL/DML in an Apex loop: each iteration consumes a DML statement from the transaction's governor limit (150 DML statements per transaction), and also risks creating tasks serially against the same case record, which can cause lock contention. With 50 comments, this Flow would consume 50 DML statements on task creation alone, in addition to the initial case save. The correct fix is to **collect all the task records to create into a collection variable inside the loop, then perform a single Record Create (bulk) after the loop exits**. **Trap:** Assuming the problem is the loop itself and trying to eliminate it by converting to a single query — the loop is necessary to process each comment; the issue is the DML placement, not the loop's existence. Or concluding the fix requires Apex — Flow's collection + bulk-create pattern handles this declaratively. **Verify:** In a sandbox with a test case containing 50 comments, run the corrected flow and inspect the Apex debug log to confirm a single DML operation creates all tasks. Confirm no governor limit exceptions appear in the log.

7. **Competent:** Raise three specific trade-offs: (1) **Login friction** — self-registration requires users to create and verify an account before submitting; for one-time, low-tech, or shared-device users (e.g., public library kiosks, elderly applicants, applicants with limited English) this is a real access barrier and a source of drop-off. 8. **Competent:** The correct approach is to **upsert on the external ID**, not insert. 11. **Competent:** A custom LWC in the utility bar can render a softphone-like UI and make REST callouts, but it lacks several capabilities that Open CTI provides as a browser-level telephony integration standard: (1) **No automatic screen pop** — Open CTI's `onCallBegin` event fires Salesforce's built-in ANI-to-record lookup and pops the matching Contact/Account/Case automatically; a custom LWC must replicate this logic manually and navigate programmatically. 12. **Competent:** A second Entitlement Process is not the right fix. Entitlement Processes define milestone types and durations — changing Business Hours for a tier is accomplished by setting the **Business Hours field directly on the Entitlement record** for VIP accounts, not by duplicating the Entitlement Process. Business Hours on the Entitlement override the org-default (and any Business Hours embedded in the Entitlement Process) for all milestones on that Entitlement. The correct approach: create a new Business Hours record for "9 AM–5 PM Pacific" (i.e., Mon–Fri 9:00–17:00 US/Pacific, which maps to 12:00–20:00 Eastern), then open each VIP account's Entitlement record and set the Business Hours field to this new record. The same Entitlement Process is reused; only the clock's reference calendar changes. **Trap:** Changing the org-default Business Hours to Pacific time — this shifts the SLA clock for every account in the org, including standard-tier accounts. Or creating a second Entitlement Process with a different Business Hours record attached at the process level — Entitlement Processes don't have a Business Hours field at the process level; the Business Hours is on the Entitlement record itself. **Verify:** After updating VIP Entitlements, create a test case for a VIP account at 6:00 PM Eastern (within Pacific business hours) and query `CaseMilestone.TargetDate` to confirm the milestone clock is running (it should be, since 6 PM Eastern = 3 PM Pacific is within Pacific hours). Do the same for a standard-tier account at 6:00 PM Eastern and confirm its milestone clock is paused (outside Eastern business hours).
